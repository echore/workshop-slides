Hallucination

It predicts the next plausible word, but it does not check whether that word is true.

predicts what sounds right → never verifies → can be confidently wrong
“Give me a reference on vaccine hesitancy in rural Canada.”
Roy, D. & Chen, M. (2019). Determinants of vaccine hesitancy in rural Canadian communities. Canadian Journal of Public Health, 110(4), 512–521.
⚠ Looks real, but no such paper exists.

A hallucination is a fluent answer that is inaccurate or invented. Fluency is not evidence of truth, so verify every fact and source.

Bias

It learns from human text, so it reflects whose knowledge is well represented, and whose is missing.

79%

A biased answer is an unreliable answer: quality can be systematically worse for some populations than others.

Data privacy

When you paste into a cloud tool, your text leaves your computer, and may be stored.

Chatbot · API
↓
A company’s serversyour text can be kept and reused
⚠ data leaves your machine
Local model
↓
Your own computerruns offline, nothing sent
✓ data stays with you

Never put patient data (PHI) into a public chatbot. For anything sensitive, use a local model.

Re-identification

“De-identified” is not the same as anonymous.

Health record, name removed
ZIP · sex · date of birth · diagnosis
+
Public voter list
name · ZIP · sex · date of birth
→
One person
re-identified by name

Latanya Sweeney linked “anonymous” hospital records to voter rolls on ZIP + sex + birth date, and re-identified the state governor’s own medical file.

Removing names isn’t enough. A few common fields can re-identify people, so treat “de-identified” data as still sensitive.

Canadian guidance

Canada has practical guidance for responsible use, the FASTER principles.

F
Fair
Don’t amplify bias; respect rights & accessibility
A
Accountable
You own the output, keep it accurate & legal
S
Secure
Protect privacy and personal information
T
Transparent
Disclose when AI was used
E
Educated
Know the limits; check for errors
R
Relevant
Right tool for the job, AI isn’t always the answer

Written for federal public servants, not a law that binds researchers. But it’s the clearest Canadian statement of what responsible use looks like.

What actually applies to you: provincial privacy law (Québec’s Law 25), health-information rules, and your institution’s research ethics board.

Algorithmic Impact Assessment

When AI helps decide about people, Canada has a tool to size up the risk.

ILittle / no impact
IIModerate
IIIHigh
IVVery high
more impact→more oversight, peer review & human sign-off required

A public federal questionnaire (65 risk + 41 mitigation questions) that scores an automated decision system and sets the safeguards it needs.

Built for government decision systems, not everyday chatbot use. But the principle travels: it becomes your concern the moment AI starts informing decisions about people: triage, prioritization, eligibility.
