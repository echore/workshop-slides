Where LLMs fall short

Four failure modes, and what to do about each.

Hallucination
IssueInvents fluent, confident answers, fake citations, wrong facts.
FixVerify every fact and source before you use it.
Bias
IssueQuality can be systematically worse for under-represented populations.
FixCheck outputs across groups; don’t assume even coverage.
No accountability
IssueThe model can’t be responsible for an error, you are.
FixKeep a human in the loop to own and sign off the output.
Privacy
IssueText pasted into a cloud tool leaves your machine and may be stored.
FixNever paste PHI. Use a local model, or protect the data first →

Today we focus on the one that’s hardest to undo: data privacy.

Protecting the data

Three ways to reduce risk, and they are not equally safe.

De-identification
Remove or mask direct identifiers (names, health numbers). Indirect ones, ZIP, age, sex, may remain.
⚠ Re-identification still possible by linkage.
Anonymization
Alter the data so re-identification risk is very low, even combined with other sources. A higher bar.
✓ Much lower risk, harder to achieve well.
Synthetic data
Artificial records that mimic the statistics of the real data, but describe no real person.
✓ No real individuals, if generated carefully.

“De-identified” is not anonymous. A few common fields, ZIP + sex + birth date, can re-identify people.

A practical playbook

Ontario’s privacy regulator gives a step-by-step method for structured data.

•Separates direct identifiers (names, IDs) from indirect ones (ZIP, age, sex) that enable linkage.
•Frames risk around an adversary trying to re-identify, and a threshold of acceptable risk.
•Lays out a process: classify variables, set a release model, measure risk, apply controls.
•Key caution: data linkage increases vulnerability, de-identification is a risk level, not a switch.

When you must share health data, follow a recognized method like this, don’t eyeball it.

Canadian guidance

Canada has practical guidance for responsible use, the FASTER principles.

F
Fair
Don’t amplify bias; respect rights & accessibility
A
Accountable
You own the output, keep it accurate & legal
S
Secure
Protect privacy and personal information
T
Transparent
Disclose when AI was used
E
Educated
Know the limits; check for errors
R
Relevant
Right tool for the job, AI isn’t always the answer

Written for federal public servants, not a law that binds researchers. But it’s the clearest Canadian statement of what responsible use looks like.

What actually applies to you: provincial privacy law (Québec’s Law 25), health-information rules, and your institution’s research ethics board.

Algorithmic Impact Assessment

When AI helps decide about people, Canada has a tool to size up the risk.

ILittle / no impact
IIModerate
IIIHigh
IVVery high
more impact→more oversight, peer review & human sign-off required

A public federal questionnaire (65 risk + 41 mitigation questions) that scores an automated decision system and sets the safeguards it needs.

Built for government decision systems, not everyday chatbot use. But the principle travels: it becomes your concern the moment AI starts informing decisions about people: triage, prioritization, eligibility.
