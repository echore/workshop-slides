Part 2 · Prompt Engineering

The principles

Prompt engineering is the discipline that has grown up around a set of best practices for making AI output more reliable, efficient, and accurate.

1

Give direction

Tell it who to be and what style to aim for.

2

Specify format

Say what structure you want the answer in.

3

Show an example

Give one worked case and it matches the pattern.

4

Evaluate quality

Check the output; ask it to rate or fix its own work.

5

Divide the work

Break big tasks into steps, done one at a time.

Give Direction

Same trial, four prompts. Watch what each added instruction changes. Green = what's new.

Prompt

“Summarize this trial in a few sentences.”

What you get

Generic recap. What the trial is, no audience in mind. ▾ hover for the real output

This is a Phase 3, randomized, double-blind trial comparing orelabrutinib, a CNS-penetrable BTK inhibitor, to placebo in people with primary progressive multiple sclerosis (PPMS). About 705 participants are randomized 2:1 (drug:placebo) and treated for roughly 30 to 60 months. The primary aim is whether orelabrutinib delays confirmed disability progression versus placebo. It is currently recruiting across the US and Europe.

+ Role

“You're an epidemiologist. Summarize this trial in a few sentences.”

What you get

Population lens. Adds the PPMS-subset context and who the results would speak to. ▾ hover for the real output

This Phase 3 trial compares orelabrutinib, an oral CNS-penetrable BTK inhibitor, with placebo in primary progressive MS, a smaller and harder-to-treat subset of the MS population. It randomizes about 705 adults aged 18 to 60 with an EDSS of 3.0 to 6.5 and documented recent progression, 2:1 to drug or placebo, for up to five years. The primary outcome is time to confirmed disability progression. From a population view, the narrow eligibility means any benefit would speak to ambulatory PPMS patients with active progression, not MS more broadly.

+ Focus

“You're an epidemiologist. Summarize this trial in under 80 words, focusing on whom the results would apply to.”

What you get

Tight, generalizability-first. Leads with whom it applies to. ▾ hover for the real output

Whom would this apply to? Enrollment is restricted to adults 18 to 60 with primary progressive MS, EDSS 3.0 to 6.5, and documented progression in the prior 24 months. So any finding would generalize to ambulatory PPMS patients with recent active progression, not to older, more disabled, or relapsing patients. It's a 2:1 placebo-controlled Phase 3 with a confirmed-disability-progression endpoint.

Swap the role

“You're a clinician advising a patient. Summarize this trial in a few sentences.”

What you get

Different content: not the population, the individual. Placebo odds, what joining involves, who's excluded. ▾ hover for the real output

This study tests whether a daily oral drug, orelabrutinib, can slow disability progression in primary progressive MS, compared with placebo. If your patient joins, there's a 2-in-3 chance of the active drug and 1-in-3 of placebo, and neither of you would know which; treatment lasts at least a year, up to five. They'd need PPMS with recent documented progression and moderate disability, and would be excluded if on blood thinners, certain interacting medications, or with other significant health conditions.

The takeaway: role shapes what counts as important, not just tone. The epidemiologist asks whom the results generalize to; the clinician asks what it means for one patient. Same record, two different reads.

Show an Example

Don't describe the format you want. Show one worked case, and the model matches it.

No example

“Extract the key details from this trial.”

What you get

A loose paragraph. Readable, but every trial comes back a different shape: nothing to line up or compare. ▾ hover for the real output

This is a Phase 3 trial studying orelabrutinib, a CNS-penetrable BTK inhibitor, for primary progressive MS. Around 705 adults are randomized 2:1 to the drug or placebo and followed for up to five years, with the main goal of seeing whether it slows confirmed disability progression. It is currently recruiting internationally.

+ One example

“Extract trial details in exactly this format. Here is a worked example: Design / Population / Intervention / Primary outcome / Status … Now extract the attached trial the same way.”

What you get

The same trial, snapped into your five fields. Nothing invented. It just fills the template. ▾ hover for the real output

Design: Phase 3, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group (2:1)
Population: Adults 18–60 with primary progressive MS, EDSS 3.0–6.5, documented recent progression; n≈705
Intervention: Orelabrutinib (oral CNS-penetrable BTK inhibitor) vs placebo
Primary outcome: Time to 12-week composite confirmed disability progression (EDSS / T25FWT / 9HPT)
Status: Recruiting

Reuse it · new trial

Same prompt, a different trial (pediatric relapsing MS, active comparator).

What you get

Identical structure, different trial. Set the pattern once, reuse it across the whole search. ▾ hover for the real output

Design: Phase 2/3, randomized, active- and placebo-controlled, multi-part (PK/PD, non-inferiority, extension)
Population: Children 10–17 with relapsing MS, EDSS 0–5.5, weight ≥25 kg; n≈240
Intervention: Ublituximab vs fingolimod (Part B); vs placebo (Part A)
Primary outcome: Ublituximab PK/PD and B-cell counts (Part A); annualized relapse rate (Part B)
Status: Recruiting

The takeaway: one worked example does what a paragraph of instructions can't: it shows the shape. Define it once, and every trial after that lands in the same fields, ready to compare.

Divide the Work

Four jobs in one prompt, or one job per prompt? Same 12 trials, one measured difference.

One overloaded prompt

“For each of these 12 trials, give: study type, a plain-language title, a one-line summary, and two reviewer questions.”

10 / 12 classified correctly

One task per prompt

1. “Classify each: interventional or observational. One word each.” 2. “Write a plain-language title for each.” 3. “Summarize each in one line.”

12 / 12 classified correctly

Both misses were traps: observational trials that name a drug or device.

NCT02677077: observational MRI study of patients already on natalizumab. Bundled, the model saw the drug name and called it interventional.

NCT03993275: observational balance study that lists a device (MindMotion GO). Same slip: the device cue overrode the design.

The takeaway: juggling four jobs at once, the model grabs a surface cue and mislabels. Give each task its own prompt and the irrelevant details fall away. Independent tasks don't belong in one prompt. (Shi et al., “Large language models can be easily distracted by irrelevant context.”)

Specify Format

Same two trials, same fields. You name the container. Green = what's new.

No format

“Summarize the key details of these two trials.”

What you get

Two prose blobs. Fine to read, but you can't scan a column or compute across trials. ▾ hover for the real output

The first trial (NCT07067463) is a Phase 3, placebo-controlled study of orelabrutinib in adults with primary progressive MS, enrolling about 705 people to test whether it slows disability progression; it is recruiting. The second (NCT07220252) is a Phase 2/3 study of ublituximab in children with relapsing MS, comparing it against fingolimod and placebo across roughly 240 participants, measuring drug levels and relapse rate; it is also recruiting.

Evaluate Quality

Don't take the first answer on faith. Make the model check its own work.

The model gives an answer. It reads well, so it's tempting to trust it as-is.

“Check your answer against the source. Flag anything unsupported.” → it catches its own gaps.

“Rate each claim high / medium / low confidence, with a reason.” → a black box becomes auditable.

Paste the findings into a different model and ask it to rate them → an independent check catches what the first was sure of.
