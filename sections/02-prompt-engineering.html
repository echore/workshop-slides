Give Direction

Same trial, four prompts. Watch what each added instruction changes. Green = what's new.

Prompt

“Summarize this trial in a few sentences.”

What you get

Generic recap. What the trial is, no audience in mind. ▾ hover for the real output

This is a Phase 3, randomized, double-blind trial comparing orelabrutinib, a CNS-penetrable BTK inhibitor, to placebo in people with primary progressive multiple sclerosis (PPMS). About 705 participants are randomized 2:1 (drug:placebo) and treated for roughly 30 to 60 months. The primary aim is whether orelabrutinib delays confirmed disability progression versus placebo. It is currently recruiting across the US and Europe.

+ Role

“You're an epidemiologist. Summarize this trial in a few sentences.”

What you get

Population lens. Adds the PPMS-subset context and who the results would speak to. ▾ hover for the real output

This Phase 3 trial compares orelabrutinib, an oral CNS-penetrable BTK inhibitor, with placebo in primary progressive MS, a smaller and harder-to-treat subset of the MS population. It randomizes about 705 adults aged 18 to 60 with an EDSS of 3.0 to 6.5 and documented recent progression, 2:1 to drug or placebo, for up to five years. The primary outcome is time to confirmed disability progression. From a population view, the narrow eligibility means any benefit would speak to ambulatory PPMS patients with active progression, not MS more broadly.

+ Focus

“You're an epidemiologist. Summarize this trial in under 80 words, focusing on whom the results would apply to.”

What you get

Tight, generalizability-first. Leads with whom it applies to. ▾ hover for the real output

Whom would this apply to? Enrollment is restricted to adults 18 to 60 with primary progressive MS, EDSS 3.0 to 6.5, and documented progression in the prior 24 months. So any finding would generalize to ambulatory PPMS patients with recent active progression, not to older, more disabled, or relapsing patients. It's a 2:1 placebo-controlled Phase 3 with a confirmed-disability-progression endpoint.

Swap the role

“You're a clinician advising a patient. Summarize this trial in a few sentences.”

What you get

Different content: not the population, the individual. Placebo odds, what joining involves, who's excluded. ▾ hover for the real output

This study tests whether a daily oral drug, orelabrutinib, can slow disability progression in primary progressive MS, compared with placebo. If your patient joins, there's a 2-in-3 chance of the active drug and 1-in-3 of placebo, and neither of you would know which; treatment lasts at least a year, up to five. They'd need PPMS with recent documented progression and moderate disability, and would be excluded if on blood thinners, certain interacting medications, or with other significant health conditions.

The takeaway: role shapes what counts as important, not just tone. The epidemiologist asks whom the results generalize to; the clinician asks what it means for one patient. Same record, two different reads.

Show an Example

Don't describe the format you want. Show one worked case, and the model matches it.

No example

“Extract the key details from this trial.”

What you get

A loose paragraph. Readable, but every trial comes back a different shape: nothing to line up or compare. ▾ hover for the real output

This is a Phase 3 trial studying orelabrutinib, a CNS-penetrable BTK inhibitor, for primary progressive MS. Around 705 adults are randomized 2:1 to the drug or placebo and followed for up to five years, with the main goal of seeing whether it slows confirmed disability progression. It is currently recruiting internationally.

+ One example

“Extract trial details in exactly this format. Here is a worked example: Design / Population / Intervention / Primary outcome / Status … Now extract the attached trial the same way.”

What you get

The same trial, snapped into your five fields. Nothing invented. It just fills the template. ▾ hover for the real output

Design: Phase 3, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group (2:1)
Population: Adults 18–60 with primary progressive MS, EDSS 3.0–6.5, documented recent progression; n≈705
Intervention: Orelabrutinib (oral CNS-penetrable BTK inhibitor) vs placebo
Primary outcome: Time to 12-week composite confirmed disability progression (EDSS / T25FWT / 9HPT)
Status: Recruiting

Reuse it · new trial

Same prompt, a different trial (pediatric relapsing MS, active comparator).

What you get

Identical structure, different trial. Set the pattern once, reuse it across the whole search. ▾ hover for the real output

Design: Phase 2/3, randomized, active- and placebo-controlled, multi-part (PK/PD, non-inferiority, extension)
Population: Children 10–17 with relapsing MS, EDSS 0–5.5, weight ≥25 kg; n≈240
Intervention: Ublituximab vs fingolimod (Part B); vs placebo (Part A)
Primary outcome: Ublituximab PK/PD and B-cell counts (Part A); annualized relapse rate (Part B)
Status: Recruiting

The takeaway: one worked example does what a paragraph of instructions can't: it shows the shape. Define it once, and every trial after that lands in the same fields, ready to compare.

Divide the Work

Three jobs in one prompt, or one job per prompt? Same 12 trials, one measured difference.

One overloaded prompt

“For each of these 12 trials, give: study type, a plain-language title, and a one-line summary.”

One task per prompt

1. “Classify each: interventional or observational. One word each.” 2. “Write a plain-language title for each.” 3. “Summarize each in one line.”

10 / 12 classified correctly
12 / 12 classified correctly

Both misses were traps: observational trials that name a drug or device.

NCT02677077: observational MRI study of patients already on natalizumab. Bundled, the model saw the drug name and called it interventional.

NCT03993275: observational balance study that lists a device (MindMotion GO). Same slip: the device cue overrode the design.

The takeaway: juggling three jobs at once, the model grabs a surface cue and mislabels. Give each task its own prompt and the irrelevant details fall away. Independent tasks don't belong in one prompt. (Shi et al., “Large language models can be easily distracted by irrelevant context.”)

Evaluate Quality

Don't trust the first answer. Independent checks catch what the author can't see.

The extraction under review (the ublituximab trial, from Show an Example)

Design: Ph2/3 RCT, active + placebo, multi-part  ·  Population: Children 10–17, RMS, EDSS 0–5.5, n≈240  ·  Intervention: Ublituximab vs fingolimod / placebo  ·  Primary outcome: PK/PD and B-cell counts (A); ARR (B)  ·  Status: Recruiting

The prompt sent to each reviewer

“You are an independent reviewer. Below is another model's extraction of a clinical trial, plus the trial's ClinicalTrials.gov record. Check every field against the record. Flag anything unsupported, incomplete, or oversimplified.”

Reviewer: ChatGPT

Independent, different model. Flags:

  • Design omits Part C; comparator structure differs by part.
  • “RCT” over-claims randomization: only Part B's comparison is verified.
  • Part B is a non-inferiority design; misses the week-96 and week-168 horizons.
  • Deeper on exclusions: prior anti-CD20, S1P modulators, recent steroids.

Reviewer: Gemini

Independent, different model. Flags:

  • Design omits the three-part structure; Part C missing entirely.
  • Population drops the Part A weight limit (25–40 kg) and the 30-day stability rule.
  • Comparators are part-specific: fingolimod in Part B only.
  • Primary outcome omits the named PK endpoints (AUC, Cmax).

Reviewer: Claude, in a fresh chat

Asked to check its own extraction, a new Claude chat caught the big gaps too (the missing Part C, the dropped weight criteria) plus two the others underplayed: “n≈240” is falsely approximate (240 is a precise target), and “RCT” is an unverified field, not a confirmed one. Self-review isn't worthless, but only when it actually checks the record instead of ratifying.

The takeaway: the author can't see its own blind spots. A different model catches them, and two different models catch different things. Even a fresh pass from the same model helps, as long as it verifies against the primary record rather than trusting the first answer.

Exercise 1 · Fix it

Rewrite a lazy screening prompt into a reproducible one.

⏱ 10 min · in teams You're screening abstracts for a systematic review. A colleague wrote the prompt below to speed it up. Rewrite it using the principles.

The prompt they wrote

“Tell me if these abstracts are relevant to my review.”

No criteria, no output shape, no reason given. Across 200 abstracts you get inconsistent calls you can't audit or report.

One strong rewrite

“You are screening abstracts for a systematic review. Include if: adult population, randomized design, reports a clinical outcome. Exclude otherwise. For each abstract return one row: ID | INCLUDE or EXCLUDE | the single criterion that decided it | confidence (high/med/low). If the abstract doesn’t report enough to judge, return UNCLEAR rather than guessing.”
roleexplicit criteriaformatreason per callno-guessing rule

There’s no single right answer, only prompts that make the decision rule explicit enough to reproduce.

Exercise 2 · Diagnose

Spot what this prompt is missing.

⏱ 10 min · in teams This prompt extracts data from 200 survey responses. It’s not terrible, but it will give messy, inconsistent output. Check it against the five principles: which are missing?

The prompt

“Read each open-ended survey answer about barriers to vaccination and tell me the main themes. Be thorough.”

It has a role-ish task and a topic. So what will go wrong across 200 rows?

What’s missing

  • Format: no fixed output shape, so every row comes back differently.
  • Example: no worked case, so the theme labels drift.
  • Fixed categories: “main themes” is open-ended; give it a set list to sort into.
  • Evaluate: nothing asks it to flag low-confidence or ambiguous answers.
add a table formatshow one examplegive categoriesask for a confidence flag

“Be thorough” is not an instruction. Structure is what makes 200 rows come back consistent.

Exercise 3 · Fix it

Make an analysis prompt auditable.

⏱ 10 min · in teams You have a cohort dataset: incident type 2 diabetes as the outcome, physical inactivity as the exposure, plus age, sex, BMI, and household income. A colleague wrote the prompt below to get a quick answer. Rewrite it using the principles.

The prompt they wrote

“Run a regression on this data and tell me what’s significant.”

You’ll get numbers. You won’t know which model it chose, which cases it dropped, or whether the assumptions hold.

One strong rewrite

“You are an epidemiologist. Fit a logistic regression predicting incident type 2 diabetes from physical inactivity, adjusting for age, sex, BMI, and household income. Report: the model specification, N included and N excluded with the reason, the adjusted odds ratio with its 95% CI for inactivity, and every covariate in the model. State the assumptions you are relying on and any you could not check. Do not drop records silently. Do not call anything significant beyond what the interval supports.”
rolespecified modelnamed covariatesreporting formatmissing-data transparencyassumption check

With analysis, the dangerous failure isn’t a messy answer. It’s a confident one you can’t audit.
